Reimagining Lynx Drive

A complete rethink of a legacy platform powering some of the world’s most advanced irrigation systems.

Lynx Drive is the number one software platform for golf irrigation management in the world. It's trusted by nearly 90% of PGA-certified golf courses globally and is considered the gold standard for precise, large-scale irrigation. Its legacy of performance and reliability made it indispensable—but also created inertia against change.

Lynx Drive is the core control interface for managing large-scale irrigation infrastructure. Used by superintendents, field techs, and managers at golf courses and sports facilities, it handles everything from zone programming and flow tracking to alert resolution and seasonal forecasting. It’s an incredibly powerful system—but power had come at the cost of usability. The platform was outdated, overwhelming, and error-prone. My mission was to lead a full-scale design transformation.

I was brought in as the senior designer to drive this transformation end-to-end: from research and product framing, through UX strategy and prototyping, to system design and handoff. This case study outlines that journey—from ambiguity to a platform that users now describe as “finally intuitive.”

We reimagined the control map as a spatial command layer—an intuitive, visual way to work in parallel with the main interface. From this single view, users could inspect, diagnose, and trigger actions in real-time.

The Problem

A trusted system showing its age.

Lynx Drive has been the backbone of large-scale irrigation management for years. It’s respected for its reliability and control, and has been deeply embedded into the daily workflows of turf professionals. But as the product matured, layers of functionality were added without rethinking how users navigated or interacted with the system. This led to bottlenecks, cognitive overhead, and hesitation from newer users who hadn’t grown with the product.

The original Lynx interface exposed every control at once—overwhelming users with dense data tables and nested menus. While powerful, it required deep institutional knowledge and constant caution to avoid missteps.

“The old mobile system felt like an afterthought. This one feels like it’s built for me.”

— Field technician, Florida

A Day in the Life: User Journey Analysis

Through shadowing superintendents and technicians across multiple golf courses, we documented every interaction with Lynx Drive throughout their day. This comprehensive journey mapping revealed not just when users needed the system, but how their mental state and environmental conditions affected their ability to interact with it effectively.

Early Morning Check-In (5:30 AM - 8:00 AM)

Dawn operations are the most critical period. Superintendents arrive before sunrise to assess overnight irrigation effectiveness and plan the day's maintenance.

  • Course Report analysis for irrigation coverage and system performance
  • Field walk-through to verify system health and identify issues
  • Review and triage overnight alerts from sensors and controllers
  • Cross-reference weather data with soil moisture readings
  • Coordinate immediate actions with early shift crews

Key Finding: Users were losing up to 45 minutes each morning jumping between 6 different system views to gather essential information. 84% maintained paper backups due to lack of trust in digital systems.

Critical Pain Point: "By the time I've checked everything in the system, I've missed something happening on the course." - Lead Superintendent

Mid-Day Operations (9:00 AM - 3:00 PM)

The most dynamic period requires constant adaptation to changing conditions while managing multiple team activities.

  • Real-time flow monitoring and adjustments
  • Program modifications based on weather and ground conditions
  • Coordination of maintenance zones with active play areas
  • Equipment diagnostics and calibration checks
  • Documentation of repairs and system adjustments
  • Integration of feedback from grounds crew observations

Key Finding: Teams were using an average of 3.7 different methods to track field work (texts, radios, paper logs, photos), creating significant information gaps and handoff issues.

Critical Pain Point: "I'm constantly switching between my phone and the office system, and information gets lost in between." - Field Technician

End-of-Day Planning (4:00 PM - 6:00 PM)

Evening preparation sets the stage for overnight success, requiring careful coordination of multiple systems and team handoffs.

  • System-wide configuration verification
  • Weather forecast integration into irrigation plans
  • Flow rate calculations and schedule optimization
  • Program sequencing for optimal coverage
  • Documentation of special conditions or zones
  • Preparation of next-day maintenance schedules

Key Finding: 92% of overnight system errors were traced to incomplete verification steps, often because the interface made it too difficult to do thorough checks.

Critical Pain Point: "There's no way to be certain everything is set correctly without checking each screen individually. It's nerve-wracking." - Lead Superintendent

This detailed journey mapping revolutionized our approach to the redesign. Instead of just modernizing the interface, we created context-aware workflows that adapt to the user's phase of day, environmental conditions, and team dynamics. The result was a 60% reduction in morning configuration time and a 75% decrease in overnight irrigation errors.

Key Insights & Opportunities

Customer Interviews / Key Insights and Opportunities

Map and Mobile are Differentiators

Map functionality, especially on mobile, is a key Lynx differentiator. Users desire system status, diagnostic info, and system update ability from mobile map.

"Smart" Suggestions and User Education

Superintendents report using only 20-30% of Lynx functionality. Explore ways (such as tooltips or animations) to enhance user discovery and understanding of Lynx features. Leverage AI to make relevant suggestions to help users save time and effort in Watering Plan updates in particular.

Team Transparency Pain Points

Superintendents desire insight into irrigation change history and more discoverable ways to limit system change access such as Advanced Set Up.

Watering Plan Efficiencies

Users want efficient, flexible ways to group, reorder, and update their watering programs on desktop, mobile, and map. They seek a Watering Plan grid that is cleaner and makes it easier to view and focus on data and options of interest.

Course Report Optimizations

Users desire a Course Report that summarizes problems more succinctly and offers more context and guidance on how to diagnose issues.

End of Day Optimizations

Users want to view the Projected Flow while iterating on their Watering Plan to track and validate changes more efficiently.

Our UX Strategy

Designing for confidence, clarity, and speed under pressure.

Our north star was to help users feel certain, fast, and in control. To get there, I proposed three UX pillars:

  • Information at a glance: Prioritize what's time-sensitive and reduce visual noise. Pull system state forward.
  • Guided flows: Let users act step-by-step, with inline guidance and guardrails for complex processes.
  • Role-tuned views: Tailor entry points and defaults based on what the user needs that day.

With our UX pillars established, I mapped each major user journey—from login through planning and resolution—directly to these strategic objectives. This ensured that every flow, wireframe, and interaction reinforced the experience we wanted to deliver: speed under pressure, clarity in complexity, and confidence across roles. These journeys informed not just screen layouts, but system structure, interaction patterns, and onboarding flows.

Rebuilding from the Field Up

This wasn’t a feature update—it was a full reboot of how irrigation software shows up in the field.

Lynx Mobile had one job: make control intuitive, immediate, and invisible. That meant eliminating all the baggage from the browser-based Lynx Cloud tool—and rethinking interaction from the hands of the technician upward.

Thumb-First, Glove-Proof:
Designed for quick, decisive actions. Every interaction could be completed with one hand in bright sun, with gloves on, and without second guessing.

Location-Aware Everything:
Zones and issues appear as overlays on the live map. Tapping any element brings up relevant actions—run water, log a note, flag a problem. The system adapts to your location and task in real time.

Offline Confidence:
Unlike Lynx Cloud, this app was built for spotty coverage. All actions queue locally and sync seamlessly when signal returns. This wasn’t a nice-to-have—it was survival in the field.

Microflows, Not Menus:
We stripped away traditional menus in favor of direct, contextual action flows. Everything is where you need it, when you need it.

Built in the Field, Literally:
We tested every prototype on turf. If it didn’t work on a bumpy cart ride or under a blazing sun—it got cut.

Impact

We replaced a liability with the most-used tool in the system.

Lynx Mobile didn’t just succeed—it erased the memory of Lynx Cloud. Within months, the app became the primary touchpoint for daily irrigation work on the ground. Usage skyrocketed. Feedback flipped from complaints to suggestions. And most importantly, crews felt seen.

  • 230% increase in daily mobile usage over the previous platform
  • 3x faster issue reporting with field-logged Notes
  • 67% drop in missed zone tasks due to live map feedback

The mobile design language born here has since shaped two other flagship products and is now the standard for all field-first tools. This wasn’t a release—it was a recalibration of what our users should expect from us.

Lynx Cloud was the past. Lynx Mobile is the blueprint.
